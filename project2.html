introduction

When I came back to Pittsburgh at the start of the school year, I noticed that there were scooters laying around various sidewalks. I had heard of Bird as a scooter rental service, but these scooters were new, with bright orange branding and the word “SPIN” on them. Naturally, when I was tasked to design a data dashboard for SPIN Scooters as a class group project, I thought it would be a cool experience as I had personally used one of the scooters myself.

The project description itself was to work in a group of 3-4 students to iteratively design a dashboard that displays information about the scooters, focusing on the needs of 2 or more personas defined by the professors. We were provided with spreadsheets of scooter data from various sources and 4 personas with different goals and usages for the data dashboard.



the problem

Given a set of personas and scooter data, we wanted to design a data dashboard that would create a pleasant experience for SPIN workers and allow them to efficiently make data-driven decisions. Our dashboard aimed to address the goals of two of the four personas.

The 4 personas we were provided with were:

  • Mia: data analyst at SPIN living in NYC
  • Elliot: policy analyst at Pittsburgh Department of Mobility and Infrastructure (DOMI)
  • Paula: accessibility and transit advocate
  • Kalen: part-time scooter redistribution worker




models & initial sketches


Before starting the formal design process, we created an affinity diagram to draw insights from the data and group together major stakeholder goals and dashboard functions. We color-coded each sticky note; white for Mia’s goals, dark pink for Kalen’s goals, yellow for Elliot’s goals, and red for Paula’s goals. We then grouped sticky notes underneath third-level broader goals to see what the personas had in common and grouped those goals under second-level dashboard functions, then finally top-level broadest dashboard functions.





Based on our affinity diagram, we created a simple current state and future model that allowed us to see how our dashboard would address limitations in the pre-dashboard state. We also thought of a few data categories that were not provided in the spreadsheets that could be useful for our personas and implemented into our dashboard elements.




Our goal with our initial sketches was to incorporate a variety of data visualizations and combine them to achieve various personas’ needs.




We decided to focus on designing the data dashboard for Mia and Kalen as we felt that they had goals that were both very quantitative and driven by similar needs, especially regarding scooter allocation and geographic data.


mid-fi prototypes



Based on our sketches, we started laying out the dashboard and putting each element into the context of our personas. We decided what features were most important to us:

  • Map view: hover over a neighborhood to view scooter information useful for both Kalen and Mia
  • Historical data: Mia can analyze data based on past time ranges to see trends and generate business insights
  • Current scooter status map + scooter status filters: Kalen can see how many scooters of each type require different services, Mia can understand scooter allocation
  • Route planning tool: Kalen can use this to see his optimal route based on either time or money

We participated in an in-class critique where we received significant feedback to improve our prototype:

  • Distinction between current and historical data
  • Clarification on what the historical data is referring to
  • Navigation bar







Having our most important features in place. we decided to build a micro design system with an expanded color palette to improve our data visualizations and overall increase aesthetics before our next iteration.

For our first two iterations, we relied solely on SPIN’s brand colors, which were bright orange, white, and black/dark grey, and this limited the breadth of our data expression. By adding two sub-colors and having a different color for alerts, we expected that we would be able to improve our dashboard design and add more variety to the dashboard elements.

We focused on having gradients for the scooter status filters, allowing Mia to generate higher-level insights by switching between statuses and allowing Kalen to understand which scooters need which service based on their status. We also implemented gradients in other data visualizations to improve aesthetics.



hi-fi prototype


We iterated on our previous prototype by implementing our micro design system, fleshing out our data visualizations further, and adding animations and interactivity, helping Mia and Kalen interact better with the dashboard. Some of the most significant features we added were an alert function for the current view, a date picker function for the historical view, and a visual redesign to incorporate our design system.




final prototype


Based on class feedback, we implemented a few more features and made changes to our dashboard:

  • New controls, features, and feedforward to interact with data
  • Changed data to be more realistic
  • Loading screen
  • Clearer visual distinction between Pittsburgh and neighborhood-specific data

takeaways

This was the first group project that I had done in Figma, and I thought it was challenging but rewarding. Some of my major takeaways were understanding how to utilize personas, gaining more proficiency in Figma, and gaining more design group work experience.

  • Personas inform and guide the design process. Throughout this project, I found myself constantly considering whether or not Mia or Kalen would benefit from certain data and functionalities, and I wanted to make sure that I found the cross-section between their goals to maximize utility for both personas.
  • Components improve work flow tremendously. I had previous experience with components in Adobe XD, but for some reason, I could not figure out how to use components in Figma. For our first few iterations, I was creating separate screens to demonstrate each animation, which was highly inefficient, so I learned how to use component variants and prototype within each component to organize my work better and reduce clutter in the Figma file.
  • Diversity within a team is essential. For this project, I worked with an undergraduate electrical and computer engineering major and a master student in human-computer interaction who had previous design work experience. We were able to leverage our backgrounds to think more critically about what data and functions would be most useful and to create a more visually pleasing design.
